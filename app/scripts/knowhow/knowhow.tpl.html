Expert Knowledge

This page describes everything a driver of an electric car needs to know.

Alternating Current vs Direct Current

Alternating Current
Alternating Current (AC) is changing the voltage from positive to negative several times per second, and is used in our power grid. AC is what power turbines produce. The voltage can be transformed to any desired value. Higher voltage allows for thinner cables, but requires more insulation.
Direct Current
Direct Current (DC) has a constant voltage. This is produced e.g. by Photovoltaics, or by converting AC to DC. It can be used to charge batteries. Converting DC to AC can also be done by using an Inverter.

Charging Station

A charging station provides one or several options to charge electric cars.

"Dumb" Power outlet ("Mode 1 and 2")
The simplest variant of a charging station just provides a power outlet with grid power. If the connection to the car is made with a simple cable, it is "Mode 1" charging (none of the currently available cars allows for this anymore). All currently available cars support "Mode 2", which means a wall connector (see further below) is requred to connect to the onbord charger, and the onboard charger has to convert the alternating current into direct current to make it useable for charging the battery.

Car Charging Outlet ("Mode 3")
A car charging outlet already contains the electronics to communicate with the car and only switches on power once the car confirms the cable is safely connected. But the power which flows to the car is the exact same AC current which is on the grid just like it would be a "dumb" power outlet. It has to be converted into direct current by the onboard charger.

Quick Charging Station
The so called quick charger stations contain charging devices which already convert the AC from the grid to DC which will be applied directly to the battery. The onboard charger of the car is bypassed. However, this requires the charging station to be compatible with the car obviously.

Wall Connector

The wall connector is used to connect the car to a wall outlet. It is required because electric cars use a handshake protocol to confirm a safe connection before any electric power is applied to the cable.

Charger

The charger is a device with a rectifier and some electronics. It converts alternating current into direct current, but has some logic to make sure the battery is charged according to the specs. Most cars have an on-board charger. However, there are also some charging stations which have built-in chargers to provide higher power and quicker charging.

(Traction-) Battery

The battery, which consists of several hundreds or thousands of cells, is used to store electric energy. Lithium Ion cells usually have a voltage of 3.7 Volts. Combining the cells into packs with some serial circuits brings the voltage of the traction battery to approximately 400V. It is called traction battery because it is used to generate forward traction of the car.

Inverter

The inverter does the opposite of the Rectifier: it converts DC to AC and can be used if an AC motor should be powered by a battery. Or if power from Photovoltaics should be fed into the grid.

Motor

The motor converts electric energy into kinetic energy (in the case of cars, it first becomes a rotating motion of the shafts and gears, but since they turn the wheels which apply the rotating energy to the street, in the end it becomes a linear motion of the car). This conversion obviously can't be 100% efficient either since some energy is "lost" in the form of heat.

Range

The range of any vehicle is depending on a number of factors. For accurate calculation results, Electricar.guru will take the following parameters into account:

Speed
Speed has a very big impact on energy consumption, especially with speeds above 60km/h. The air drag increases exponentially with higher speeds, which is why sustaining e.g. 200km/h requires an incredible amount of energy. This can easily be confirmed by measuring the amount of fuel a normal gasoline car uses at that speed.
Driving Style
Just like gasoline cars, also electric cars use more energy when driving more agressive. Also, every acceleration/deceleration cycles uses more energy than just driving with the respective average speed. Since electric cars can feed energy back to the battery, stop-and-go traffic doesn't have such a big impact on range like in gasoline cars, but recuperation is only about 75% efficient. That's 75% better than a gasoline car, but still the overall energy consumption is higher than in normal fluent driving.
Terrain
The terrain is an important factor for the range of any vehicle because overcoming the earths gravity takes energy as well. There is a rule of thumb vor electric cars: 1km climbing takes about the same amount as if the car would drive for 40km extra on flat terrain.
Car Aerodynamics and Vehicle Size
The aerodynamics of a car is especially important for driving speeds above 60 km/h since the air drag increases exponentially with higher speeds.
Vehicle weight
Heavier cars have a higher rolling drag coefficient, which is why heavier cars use more energy for cruising. Also, accelerating requires more power to overcome the inertia. Thanks to recuperation, while decelerating, it is possible to capture up to 75% of that energy and store it back in the battery, and this is even a better value than light cars have (probably around 70%). But still, those missing 25% will be a higher absolute number than the missing 30% of light vehicles.
Weather
Both heating and cooling the cabin draws energy from the battery. The bigger the delta between outside and desired cabin temperature (21Â° Celsius is assumed), the more energy will be needed to adjust the temperature. This is why in cold winter temperatures, it is recommended to pre-heat the cabin while the car is still plugged into a power outlet. Modern electric cars use shore power instead of power from the battery if it is plugged in. Rain or snow change the rolling and drag coefficients, which means energy consomption will be higher than in dry conditions.

Basic Terms

A
Ampere is the SI unit to measure electrical current. A higher value requires a thicker cable.
AC
Alternating Current (see descreption on the left)
Brick protection
Not a scientific term. If a battery is "bricked", it means it is dead due to very low discharge and therefore doesn't any help more than a brick. A low discharge may severely damage the battery, which is why most manufacturers use some software logic to prevent such a discharge.
C
Coulomb is an SI unit to measure charge. Charging a 22kWh battery with 22kW is 1C. Charging it with 44kW is 2C. With current battery technology, 2C is just about the maximum recommended factor. Anything above it will shorten the battery life.
Charging loss
This term may be a bit confusing since according to physics, energy can not actually be lost. It can only be transformed. But the purpose of a charger in combination with a battery is to convert electric energy to chemical energy. And this purpose is only met usually to 90-95%. The rest of the electric energy is transformed into thermal energy (aka heat). Depending on the charger, it may even be required to cool the devices during the charging procedure, which consumes power as well.
Example: When charging at a 50kW outlet, if there are 10% charging loss, the air around the car will be heated up as if there would be a 5kW heater. So after charging for 1 hour, the battery will only have 45kWh more energy stored instead of 50kWh more.
DC
Direct Current (see descreption on the left)
SoC
State of Charge is a percentage value of the battery capacity.
kW
Kilowatt is electrical power. If a 22kWh battery should be charged in 2 hours, it requires an 11kW charger (simplified).
kWh
Kilowatthour is electrical energy. When using an 11kW charger for 2 hours, 22kWh will be charged (simplified).
Ω
Ohm is the SI unit to measure resistance. A higher value will make the voltage drop more over a given distance.
Recuperation
If the motor of an electric car is used as a generator to convert kinetic energy back to electric energy, it is called recuperation. The energy which is produced by the motor/generator is fed back to the traction battery for later use.
V
Volt is the SI unit to measure voltage. A higher value requires more insulation.
W
Watt is the SI unit to measure electrical power. For direct current, the voltage (V) multiplied by the current (A) is equal to the Watt value. AC has an additional power factor between W and VA, but lets neglect that for simplicitys sake.
